<h1>Received an Income Tax Notice Under Section 143(1)? Here's What It Means and What You Should Do</h1>

<p>Many taxpayers panic after receiving an email or SMS stating that an <strong>Income Tax Intimation under Section 143(1)</strong> has been issued. The word <em>"notice"</em> often creates unnecessary fear, but in most cases, a Section 143(1) communication is simply an automated intimation sent after your Income Tax Return (ITR) has been processed.</p>

<p>If you've recently filed your ITR for Assessment Year (AY) 2026-27 and received a communication from the Income Tax Department, don't panic. This guide explains what Section 143(1) means, how to read the intimation, why it is issued, what actions you should take, and when professional assistance may be helpful.</p>

<div class="alert alert-success">

<strong>Good News</strong>

<p class="mb-0">
A Section 143(1) intimation is <strong>not the same as a scrutiny notice under Section 143(2)</strong>. In most cases, it simply confirms that your return has been processed. The result may be one of the following:
</p>

<ul class="mt-2 mb-0">
<li>Your return is accepted exactly as filed.</li>
<li>You are eligible for an income tax refund.</li>
<li>A tax demand has been raised because the department's calculations differ from your return.</li>
</ul>

</div>

<hr>

<h2>What is Section 143(1) of the Income Tax Act?</h2>

<p>Section 143(1) provides for the preliminary processing of every Income Tax Return after it is filed. The Centralised Processing Centre (CPC), Bengaluru, automatically compares the information provided in your return with records available to the Income Tax Department such as:</p>

<ul>

<li>Form 26AS</li>

<li>Annual Information Statement (AIS)</li>

<li>Taxpayer Information Summary (TIS)</li>

<li>TDS reported by employers</li>

<li>Advance tax and self-assessment tax payments</li>

<li>Income declared in your ITR</li>

<li>Deductions and exemptions claimed</li>

</ul>

<p>Once the comparison is completed, CPC issues an intimation summarising the department's calculations and the final tax position. This is a routine computerized process and does not automatically mean your return has been selected for detailed scrutiny. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}</p>

<h2>Is Section 143(1) a Tax Notice?</h2>

<div class="alert alert-warning">

<strong>Not Usually.</strong>

<p class="mt-2 mb-0">

Although taxpayers commonly call it a "143(1) Notice", it is technically an <strong>Intimation</strong>. It is generated automatically after processing your return and should not be confused with scrutiny proceedings.

</p>

</div>

<p>Only if a tax demand is raised or adjustments have been made should you carefully review the differences and determine whether further action is required.</p>

<h2>What Should You Do After Receiving a Section 143(1) Intimation?</h2>

<ol>

<li>Download the PDF from your registered email or Income Tax portal.</li>

<li>Read the comparative statement carefully.</li>

<li>Check every tax credit shown.</li>

<li>Compare it with Form 26AS and AIS.</li>

<li>Verify refund or tax demand.</li>

<li>Keep all supporting documents safely.</li>

<li>If differences exist, determine whether they are genuine before making any payment.</li>

</ol>

<div class="alert alert-primary">

Many taxpayers immediately pay the tax demand without checking whether the department has correctly considered TDS, advance tax or self-assessment tax. Always reconcile your records first before taking any action. Recent guidance also recommends responding promptly where required, particularly if a demand has been raised. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

<h2>How to Read Your Section 143(1) Intimation</h2>

<p>The PDF issued by the Centralised Processing Centre (CPC) may appear complicated at first because it compares multiple tax calculations side by side. However, understanding each column makes it much easier to identify why a refund or tax demand has been generated.</p>

<div class="table-responsive">

<table class="table table-bordered">

<thead class="table-dark">

<tr>

<th>Column</th>

<th>Meaning</th>

</tr>

</thead>

<tbody>

<tr>

<td>As Provided by Taxpayer</td>

<td>Figures exactly as filed in your Income Tax Return.</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>As Computed Under Section 143(1)</td>

<td>Income Tax Department's calculations after processing.</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>Difference</td>

<td>Any variation between your return and departmental records.</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>Tax Payable</td>

<td>Additional amount payable, if any.</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td>Refund</td>

<td>Amount refundable if excess tax has been paid.</td>

</tr>

</tbody>

</table>

</div>

<p>Most taxpayers should carefully compare every figure before accepting a demand or assuming a refund amount is correct.</p>

<h2>What If You Agree with the Tax Demand?</h2>

<div class="alert alert-warning">

If, after reviewing your records, you find that the Income Tax Department's calculations are correct, you should pay the outstanding demand within the prescribed time through the Income Tax e-Filing Portal.

</div>

<p>After making payment:</p>

<ul>

<li>Save the challan receipt.</li>

<li>Verify the payment reflects correctly.</li>

<li>Respond to the outstanding demand on the Income Tax portal if required.</li>

<li>Keep all documents for future reference.</li>

</ul>

<h2>Can You File a Rectification Request?</h2>

<p>Yes.</p>

<p>If the demand arises because of an apparent mistake in processing, taxpayers may be eligible to submit a <strong>Rectification Request under Section 154</strong>.</p>

<div class="alert alert-info">

Examples include:

<ul class="mt-2 mb-0">

<li>TDS credit not considered</li>

<li>Advance tax omitted</li>

<li>Duplicate income reported</li>

<li>Calculation mistake</li>

<li>Incorrect interest computation</li>

<li>Data mismatch after revised reporting</li>

</ul>

</div>

<p>Rectification should only be filed after carefully reviewing the reason for the adjustment.</p>
